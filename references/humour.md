# Humour

> Restaurant ops content is boring. Your job is to make it readable. Every blog post lands at least once.
> Read this file **before** writing a single sentence.

---

## The north-star

Brock is a bar GM who's seen everything. Owners trying to take from the tip pool. Bartenders pouring 1.7oz and calling it 1.5. Cooks comping plates to friends and forgetting to ring it. The walk-in seal failing for three days and nobody noticing. Variance reports that lie because the recipe costs are six months stale.

He's not bitter. He's amused. He's the operator who's been running the math while the owner's at the back table — and he's writing the post because if more operators ran the math, fewer of them would be quietly closing their doors.

Voice register: **late-night, deadpan, has-seen-everything, dryly amused.** Never punching down. Always punching at the system, at the math, at himself for the times he was the new bartender pouring 1.7.

---

## The opening rule

If the first 75 words don't include a specific operator moment, a self-aware aside, or a deadpan observation about how this stuff actually plays out — rewrite them. We are not a textbook. We are a guy who's been on the bar for five years explaining what variance reports actually catch.

---

## Five rules for humour in every post

### 1. Self-deprecation is the cheapest gold.

Brock spent three of his five years running variance reports because nobody else was. He poured 1.7oz when he started. He took a tip-pool conversation badly the first time he gave it. **Lead with the times he got it wrong.** That's the entire trust contract with the reader.

- "I poured 1.7oz when I started. I called it 1.5oz. The number didn't catch me — the senior bartender did."
- "The first time I had to tell an owner he couldn't take from the pool, I made it twice as awkward as it needed to be."
- "I ran my first variance report three months too late. By then the leak was already ten thousand bucks."

### 2. Punch at the industry. Never at the operator reading.

Operators reading this site are working a 14-hour Saturday. They don't need a hot take roasting them. They need to feel less alone.

Free targets:
- Toast / Square / R365 marketing ("free calculator" behind 3 forms)
- Owners who aren't engaged ("the back-table owner" archetype)
- AI-generated SEO slop articles (the competition)
- POS systems that crash on busy nights
- Vendors who short-ship
- Old bar managers who insist 2oz pours build customer loyalty

Never targets:
- The reader
- The reader's staff
- Specific named operators except with explicit consent (Felix, Joe — already approved per `references/stories.md`)

### 3. Specific beats general. Always.

Generic: "Most operators don't track their food cost weekly."
Specific: "I knew an operator who pulled food cost twice a year — at tax time, when his accountant made him. The first month he ran it weekly, the leak he found was paying his rent."

Specific lands because it's an actual thing that actually happened. Generic sounds like every other restaurant blog post.

### 4. Deadpan industry observations.

The setup is real. The punchline is the unstated thing the operator already knows.

- "The 80/20/30 rule was finalised in November 2021 and vacated by the Fifth Circuit in August 2024. That's three years of operators changing their tip policies twice. Get a labor attorney."
- "Buyback culture: a slow Tuesday's loyalty program. A busy Saturday's silent margin leak. Same hand. Different night."
- "The bartender who pours by feel and the bartender who skims a bottle a week look identical to the variance report. Variance tells you something is wrong. It doesn't tell you which kind of wrong."

### 5. The sign-off has to land.

Last sentence makes the reader smile. Not:

> "Use the calculator weekly to maintain healthy margins."

But:

> "The math is the easy part. The discipline of running the math weekly — and refusing the 'just this once' exception when an owner asks for a cut — that's the entire game."

Or:

> "Run the variance report weekly. The number doesn't lie. The number doesn't have feelings about your regulars. The number just tells you whether you're still in business in twelve months."

## Anti-patterns — never do these

- **Exclamation marks.** Operator humour works deadpan. The bar GM at end-of-Tuesday doesn't shout punchlines.
- **Emojis.** Not in body, not in headings, not in CTAs.
- **Laughing at the operator.** They're working hard with thin margins. Punch at the system, not the reader.
- **Forced quirk.** If a joke isn't lived-experience or industry-aimed, cut it.
- **Same joke twice.** If you made a tip-pool quip in section 2, don't make another in section 8. Pick a different muscle.
- **Trying to be a stand-up.** Brock is amused, not performing. One line per 400 words is enough.
- **Generic restaurant humour.** Avoid clichés about "the kitchen never sleeps" / "running on coffee" / "the front-of-house drama." Specific operational moments only.

---

## Frequency — how much is too much?

**Every 400–600 words, one moment.** One self-deprecation, one deadpan industry observation, one specific incident. Not every paragraph — that's exhausting. But frequent enough that the reader's face moves at least every two minutes.

A 2,000-word post should land roughly:

- **3–4** specific operator moments (Cosmos / Felix / Joe / "I knew an operator who…")
- **1–2** self-deprecating asides (something Brock got wrong)
- **2–3** deadpan industry observations
- **1** sign-off that lands

---

## The vibe check

Before shipping, read it aloud in your head. Does it sound like:

✓ A bar GM at end-of-Tuesday explaining variance reports to a new operator
✗ A textbook
✗ A LinkedIn motivational post
✗ A SaaS product page
✗ An AI being clever

If it leans textbook, sprinkle more lived experience. If it leans clever, dial it back. Target: **bar GM at end-of-Tuesday, one-and-a-half beers in, specific incidents on every page.**

## Example openings — copy the energy, not the lines

> I started behind the bar at Cosmos Taverna and worked my way up to GM over five years. In that time the bar program ran the full range — busy, slow, profitable, leaking, every kind of leaking, and twice a quarter, mysteriously profitable for reasons that always turned out to be a vendor short-shipping us.

> The first time an owner asked me to throw a couple twenties from the tip pool his way "for being a good night," I took it badly. Not because I'm a stickler. Because federal law is unambiguous, and the conversation I had to give him was the conversation that kept the bar from being in the next DOL enforcement bulletin.

> Variance reports are the metric most operators learn the hard way. I learned it the hard way. We were running a healthy beverage program on paper. Sales were strong. Margins looked fine. Then I did a real bottle count for the first time in months, lined it up against the POS report, and found a gap I couldn't explain.

Each one: lived experience first, specific number or detail, dry observation, move on. That's the pattern.
