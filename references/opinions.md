# Opinions — Hot takes Brock has

> One strong opinion per post **maximum**. Always backed by a number, a cited source, or lived experience. Hot takes without evidence are just AI noise.

---

## 1. Most restaurant calculators are lead-gen forms in disguise.

Toast, Square, Restaurant365, MarginEdge, BinWise — every one of them has a "free calculator" gated behind email signup. The calculator itself isn't the product; you're the product. Three days after you give them your email, a sales rep is in your inbox about a $400/month POS.

**Backing:** the entire `RestaurantCalcs` site exists as the counterfactual. Free, no signup, no email gate, no upsell. Used as a recurring frame in opening + closing of posts.

---

## 2. Theft is the *last* suspect in variance investigations, not the first.

Operators see a variance gap and assume theft. Nine times out of ten it's training, vendor short-ships, comp creep, or stale recipe costs. Theft happens — but treating every variance spike as theft poisons team trust faster than the variance itself ever costs you.

**Backing:** Brock's five-year experience running variance at Cosmos. He caught one bartender stealing in five years — and caught them by accident, not because variance flagged it. Variance flagged the *other* leaks every week.

**Cite alongside:** the standard investigation order — comp records → recipe specs → receiving → inventory miscount → pour discipline → *then* theft.

---

## 3. Hours-weighted is the only tip pool method that survives a Saturday rush.

Equal split feels fair on paper but punishes the senior bartender carrying the rush. Points-weighted feels fair until you're defending an org chart at 2am. Hours-weighted is the simplest and the easiest to defend when somebody on the team thinks the pool is rigged.

**Backing:** five years of post-shift tip-pool conversations. The math doesn't matter as much as the *transparency*. Hours-weighted is transparent because anyone can recount it.

---

## 4. Generosity without accounting is the leading cause of indie restaurant closure.

The closures that hurt aren't the ones from bad food or bad service. They're the ones from operators who fed everyone for free, comped everything, ran a great room — and ran out of money. Felix at Flaco's. The version of every great cook who didn't run the math.

**Backing:** Felix's story (`stories.md` #2). Industry comp/waste budget benchmarks (3–5% of food sales — see `stats.md`).

---

## 5. Discipline without team trust is a slower closure, not a different one.

The opposite-pole cautionary tale. Joe at Manolos counted every ounce, weighed every portion, took broken dishes out of tip-outs. Manolos is still open. The staff hated him, turnover was punishing, customers felt the tension. That's a slower leak — but the same closure if it goes on long enough.

**Backing:** Joe's story (`stories.md` #3). Hospitality industry turnover benchmarks (variable but 70%+ in QSR per BLS, lower in tip-credit FOH).

---

## 6. Most operators undercount labor by 15–25% by tracking only wages.

Wages aren't labor cost. Labor cost is wages + payroll taxes (FICA 7.65%) + FUTA + SUTA + workers comp + benefits + PTO accrual. Industry rule of thumb: gross wages × 1.20–1.30 for fully-loaded labor. Operators who skip this look "lean" on labor for a year, then wonder why margin's gone.

**Backing:** Restaurant365, 7shifts, Sling guidance. NRA *Uniform System of Accounts for Restaurants*.

---

## 7. The 80/20/30 rule is dead, but that doesn't make tip credits simpler.

The DOL's 80/20/30 rule was finalized November 2021, vacated by the Fifth Circuit August 2024 in *Restaurant Law Center v. DOL*. Operators who built policies around the 80/20/30 rule for two-and-a-half years now have to re-engineer. The underlying FLSA "unrelated work" rule still applies — bartenders doing inventory for four hours at the end of a shift is still a tip-credit problem.

**Backing:** *Restaurant Law Center v. DOL* (5th Cir. Aug 2024). DOL Fact Sheet #15.

---

## 8. Concept-aware benchmarks beat generic "industry standard."

A QSR running 22% labor cost gets flagged "Lean" by every full-service-tuned tool — when 22% is actually healthy for QSR. Same with food cost: pizza concepts run 22–28%, fine dining runs 30–35%. The number that matters isn't "industry standard." It's the band for *your* concept type.

**Backing:** Toast, R365, NRA concept-specific tables. RestaurantCalcs labor calc has concept selector for this exact reason.

---

## 9. Owners not engaged in the bar program is the root cause of bar drift.

When the owner's not pulling weekly variance, not auditing pour specs, not signing off on comp policies — the program drifts. New bartenders pour by feel. Comps stop being rung. Vendor short-ships go unnoticed. Six months later there's a four-point gap and nobody knows when it started. The GM either runs interference or watches it bleed.

**Backing:** Brock's lived experience at Cosmos. Recurring throughline of the operator stories on `/calculators/liquor-pour-cost/`, `/calculators/labor-cost/`, and the bar-pour-cost blog post.

---

## 10. Charm pricing is rounding *to the nearest* .95, not always rounding up.

$15.00 → $14.95 (closer than $15.95). $15.50 → $15.95. The "always round up" approach overcharges customers and signals a lazy menu price. RestaurantCalcs' menu-pricing calc fixes this.

**Backing:** consumer pricing research. Toast pricing strategy posts.

---

## 11. The math is the easy part.

Recurring frame, shows up in almost every post. The discipline of running the math weekly — not the calculation itself — is what separates operators who renew leases from operators who don't.

**Backing:** entire site thesis. Cited inline as needed.

## What never to do with opinions

- **Don't invent statistics** to back an opinion. If the number isn't in `stats.md` or a citable industry source, hedge or skip.
- **Don't claim opinions are universal.** Frame as "in five years on the bar I saw…" or "the operators I've watched succeed…" not "all operators…"
- **Don't roast specific named operators except Cosmos / Felix / Joe with the framing already established.**
- **Don't punch at restaurant workers.** They're working hard. Punch at the system, the math, the absentee owners, the SaaS marketing teams.
- **Don't pile on opinions.** One per post max. A post that takes seven hot takes reads as ranting. A post that takes one hot take and backs it with a story reads as authoritative.
