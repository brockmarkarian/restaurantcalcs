A party of ten booked our best Saturday slot, 7pm, two tables we pushed together by the window. They never showed. No call. We held those seats forty-five minutes before we gave up, and by then the walk-ins we turned away were already down the street eating somewhere else. That one no-show cost us a lot more than the empty chairs, and it is the reason I started taking deposits in the first place.

So how much should you actually charge? Here is the short answer, then the math that gets you there.

## The short answer

Most restaurants charge **$10 to $25 per person** for a no-show or a late cancellation, or they hold a **full prepaid deposit** on large parties and prime-time bookings. Fine dining and tasting menus go higher, usually **$50 to $100 per seat**. The fee gets set at booking, charged to a card on file, and triggered by a clear cancellation window, normally 24 to 48 hours.

That is the range. The right number for your room depends on what an empty seat actually costs you, so start there.

## What a no-show actually costs

The fee is not a punishment. It is supposed to cover what you lose, and most operators lowball it because they only count the empty chairs.

> **No-show cost = (covers x average check) + lost walk-in revenue from the held table**

Run my party of ten on a $32 average check. That is **$320** in covers gone. But the real damage is the second half. We held two prime tables through the busiest ninety minutes of the week and turned away walk-ins who would have filled them, maybe twice over. Call it another $300 to $500 in tables we could have turned. One no-show, north of $600 on the night, plus whatever we prepped for a ten-top that went in the trash.

That is why a $50 deposit on a big party is not greedy. It barely covers it.

## How to size the fee

Tie the number to your average check and your no-show rate, not to what feels fair. Reservation no-show rates run **10 to 20%** for most full-service restaurants, higher on holidays and weekends. If one in five big parties ghosts you and each one costs $600, you are bleeding real money every month you do not have a policy.

Here is where I land most concepts:

| Party size | Suggested deposit or fee | When to require it |
|---|---|---|
| 1 to 5 | None, or a card hold on prime-time slots | Saturdays, holidays |
| 6 to 9 | $10 to $15 per person, or a card hold | Always on weekends |
| 10 to 15 | $20 to $25 per person deposit | Always |
| 16 and up | Full prepay, or $25+ per person | Always |
| Tasting menu, NYE, holidays | Full prepay regardless of size | Always |

The deposit can apply to the final bill when they show, which makes it an easy sell. They are not paying extra, they are just putting skin in the game.

## Card hold vs hard charge

Two ways to do this, and they land very differently with guests.

A **card hold** authorizes the card at booking but only charges it if they no-show or cancel late. Softer. Most guests are fine with it because nothing actually moves unless they flake. This is what I default to for 6 to 12 tops.

A **hard charge** takes a real prepaid deposit up front. Stronger protection, but it creates friction at booking and you will lose a few reservations over it. I save this for the bookings that hurt the most when they vanish: 16-plus, buyouts, holidays, tasting menus.

Be honest with yourself here. Charging somebody's card after the fact makes enemies, and they leave reviews about it. A clearly communicated hold almost never does, because they agreed to it and they know the rules going in. The policy only works if nobody is surprised by it.

## The policy language that holds up

The fee is worthless if it is buried or vague. Three things make it stick:

1. **State it at booking.** On the reservation page, in the confirmation text, and again in the reminder. "Parties of 10+ require a $20 per person deposit, refundable up to 48 hours before your reservation."
2. **Give a real window.** 24 to 48 hours to cancel free. Reasonable windows hold up; "no cancellations ever" gets disputed and reversed.
3. **Honor it both ways.** If you cancel on them, or you cannot seat them within a reasonable time of their reservation, the deposit comes back. That is the part that keeps it fair and keeps the reviews clean.

OpenTable, Resy, and Tock all support card holds and deposits natively, so the mechanics are not the hard part. Deciding to enforce it is.

## What to do today

Pull your reservation no-show rate for the last month if your system tracks it. Multiply your no-show count by your average check, then add a conservative number for the tables you held and could not turn. That total is what your missing policy costs you. Set a deposit on parties of 10-plus first, since that is where the damage concentrates, communicate it clearly at booking, and apply it to the final bill when they show. Start there before you touch the smaller tables.
