When I first ran labor cost percentage for my own restaurant, I divided gross wages by sales and got 24%. Healthy by any benchmark. Six months later the bookkeeper showed me the labor line on the P&L: 32%. I thought he had made a mistake. He had not. I had been reading the wrong number for six months.

The 24% was wages. The 32% was the cost of actually employing people. Industry calls the difference payroll burden, and most operators leave it out of their weekly tracking.

## The formula (the version that matches your P&L)

> **Labor Cost % = (Gross Wages + Salaries + Payroll Burden) ÷ Total Sales × 100**

The headline version on Toast and 7shifts blogs gives you just wages over sales. That number is useful for staffing decisions, useless for understanding your P&L. The fully loaded version below is what shows up on the income statement.

**What's in payroll burden:**

- **FICA** (Social Security + Medicare): 7.65% of wages, employer share
- **FUTA** (federal unemployment): 0.6% on first $7,000 per employee
- **SUTA** (state unemployment): 1-6% depending on state and your experience rating
- **Workers comp insurance:** 3-7% of payroll for restaurant industry (NCCI class codes 9082 / 9083)
- **Health insurance contribution:** 0-15% of wages depending on plan and your match
- **Paid time off accrual:** 2-5% if you offer PTO/sick
- **401(k) match:** 0-3% if you offer

The all-in burden multiplier for a typical independent restaurant lands in the **25-35%** range. Higher in California, New York, Washington (mandatory paid sick + high SUTA + high workers comp). Lower in states like Texas, Florida, Tennessee. A $20/hour line cook in California with workers comp at 5%, SUTA at 4%, FICA at 7.65%, and 4% health stipend costs you about $20 × 1.27 = **$25.40/hour** fully loaded. In Texas with lower SUTA and no mandatory paid sick: $20 × 1.18 = **$23.60/hour**.

The point: a "we're paying $20/hour" decision is really a "we're paying $24-27 of labor expense" decision. Build the burden multiplier into your hiring math or every wage decision is off by 20-35%.

## A worked example

Casual full-service restaurant, one-week period.

**Gross hourly wages:** $11,400
**Salaried management (weekly portion):** $2,100
**Subtotal wages:** $13,500

**Payroll burden** (28% blended for this operation):
- FICA: $13,500 × 7.65% = $1,033
- SUTA + FUTA: $13,500 × 4.2% = $567
- Workers comp: $13,500 × 5% = $675
- Health stipend: $13,500 × 5% = $675
- PTO accrual: $13,500 × 2% = $270
- **Total burden: $3,220** (23.9% effective)

**Fully loaded labor:** $13,500 + $3,220 = **$16,720**

**Sales for the week:** $82,000

**Headline labor cost:** $13,500 ÷ $82,000 = **16.5%** (the misleading number)

**Fully loaded labor cost:** $16,720 ÷ $82,000 = **20.4%** (the real number)

The 4-point gap is what separates the staffing-decision number from the P&L number. Tracking only the headline understates your true labor by ~25%.

## Labor % vs SPLH: which one to trust

Labor cost percentage and sales-per-labor-hour (SPLH) answer different questions.

**Labor cost %** tells you the share of revenue going to labor. Useful for setting a target and tracking against P&L.

**SPLH** = total sales ÷ total labor hours. Tells you the productivity of each scheduled hour. Useful for shift-level scheduling decisions.

A weekly labor % of 28% can hide a Saturday night at 38% if your slower weekdays are running 22%. The labor % averages it out. SPLH catches the Saturday problem because the productivity number degrades the same week, in the same shift, before payroll closes.

The right discipline: track labor % weekly, track SPLH by shift. The first tells you whether the operation is sustainable. The second tells you which shifts to fix.

For the SPLH math in detail, see [Sales Per Labor Hour: The Number That Tells You Who To Cut First](/blog/splh-shift-cuts/).

## The 30% benchmark across segments

Industry-standard labor cost benchmark for full-service restaurants: **28-32%** (NRA 2024). Some specific segment medians:

- **QSR / fast food:** 25-30% (high check velocity, low wage rates)
- **Fast casual:** 28-32%
- **Casual full-service:** 30-35%
- **Family restaurant:** 30-35%
- **Fine dining:** 32-38% (more BOH, skilled positions, higher service ratio)
- **Steakhouse:** 32-36%
- **Coffee shop / café:** 28-34%
- **Pizza (delivery + takeout):** 25-30% (lean BOH, high check velocity)
- **Sports bar / pub:** 28-34%
- **Bar-only (no kitchen):** 18-24%

The latest published NRA medians: full-service **36.5%**, limited-service **31.7%** (NRA 2024 Industry Factbook, based on 2024 operator survey data). Both are at historical highs driven by post-pandemic wage compression.

## State-by-state wrinkles

Labor cost varies more by state than by concept.

- **California, New York, Washington, Oregon:** Higher base wages, mandatory paid sick, no tip credit. Labor cost typically runs 4-8 points higher than national average.
- **Texas, Florida, Georgia:** Lower base wages, tip credit allowed, lower SUTA. Labor cost typically runs 2-4 points below national average.
- **Tipped employees:** Whether your state allows a tip credit ($2.13/hr base federal, up to $5.12/hr credit) changes labor cost dramatically. A server paid $7.25 in a state with no tip credit costs the operator twice what a server paid $2.13 in a tip-credit state.

This is why segment benchmarks alone can mislead. A California fine-dining steakhouse at 38% labor might be lean. A Texas QSR at 30% labor might be over-staffed.

## Where the leak usually is

Labor cost over-runs are almost never about wage rates. They are almost always about *hours*. The two most common patterns:

**1. Same-time arrivals.** Everyone scheduled to start at 4pm for a 5:30pm dinner rush. The first 45 minutes is everyone standing around prepping the prep that wasn't done. Stagger 4:00, 4:30, 5:00 arrivals and you save 2-4 hours of labor per night per shift.

**2. Slow shift over-staffing.** A Tuesday lunch with 4 servers when 2 would handle the volume. Operators schedule "what feels safe" instead of "what the forecast supports." A weekly labor over-run of 8 hours across slow shifts is $200-300 of fully loaded labor that produced no revenue.

The fix is forecast-based scheduling. Pull your last 4 weeks of same-day-of-week sales. Set a labor target as a percentage. Build the schedule against the forecast, not against habit.

## What to do today

Pull last week's payroll. Add a 28% burden multiplier (or the right number for your state). Divide by sales. That is your real labor cost. If it differs from what you have been reporting by more than 2 points, the gap is the cost category you have been understating, and it is showing up as "net margin lower than I expected" on the monthly P&L.

The fix is not a software purchase. It is the discipline of multiplying gross wages by 1.28 every week before you write the number down.
