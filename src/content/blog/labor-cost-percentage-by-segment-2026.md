The 2024 NRA Industry Factbook landed with the news every operator already felt in their bones: labor cost is at historical highs. Full-service median **36.5%**. Limited-service median **31.7%**. Both are 4-5 points higher than five years ago. This is the version of those benchmarks that tells you where to actually target.

## The latest NRA medians

**Full-service restaurants:** 36.5% median labor cost
**Limited-service restaurants:** 31.7% median labor cost

These numbers are pulled from the NRA's 2024 operator survey, the largest industry-wide cross-section published. "Median" matters here. The arithmetic mean is closer to 38.5% (the distribution is right-skewed by struggling operators). The median tells you the middle operator. Half the industry runs higher, half lower.

The historical context: pre-pandemic (2019), full-service median was 32.4%, limited-service was 28.2%. The 4-point shift since 2019 is the residue of minimum wage increases, post-pandemic labor scarcity, and the wage compression where line cooks now earn within 10% of what supervisors do.

## Why 2026 labor costs are at historical highs

Three structural shifts:

**1. State-level minimum wage increases.** California $20/hour for fast food workers (AB 1228, effective April 2024), New York $16/hour statewide, Washington $16.66 statewide. These set floor effects that cascade up the wage scale. When QSR base hits $20, casual full-service has to pay $22-25 to attract talent, fine dining has to pay $28-32.

**2. Tipped wage compression.** States banning tip credit (California, Nevada, Oregon, Washington, Minnesota, Alaska, Montana) force full minimum wage payment to tipped employees. Servers earning $4 cash + $20 tips became servers earning $20 cash + $15 tips, with the operator absorbing the full wage cost.

**3. Post-pandemic labor scarcity.** Restaurant industry workforce is still 8-12% below 2019 levels (BLS data through 2024). Fewer workers chasing the same number of shifts means wage pressure.

The result: labor cost shifted from "12-14% of revenue for hourly + 4-6% for management" structures to "16-19% hourly + 5-7% management" structures. Same operations, same volume, 4 points more labor.

## By segment (2026 operating targets)

The NRA medians cover broad categories. The right *target* for your specific segment is the median minus 4-6 points (operators above median are struggling; below are healthy).

**QSR / fast food:** 25-30% target (median: 31.7%)

Volume-driven. The right number depends on average ticket. A QSR doing $400/hour in sales with 4 staff at $18 = $72 labor = 18% labor cost. A QSR doing $180/hour with 3 staff at $18 = $54 labor = 30% labor cost. Same staffing pattern, very different outcome.

**Fast casual:** 28-32% target (median: 33-34%)

Counter service with table delivery. Slightly higher labor than QSR because of the runner role and the higher service expectation.

**Casual full-service:** 30-35% target (median: 36.5%)

The largest category by operator count. Wide spread. Healthy operations land 30-33%, struggling at 38-42%.

**Family restaurant / diner:** 30-35% target (median: 36-37%)

Breakfast service drives labor higher because turn time is faster but check averages are lower.

**Fine dining:** 32-38% target (median: 38-40%)

Higher kitchen staffing ratio (skilled BOH), higher service ratio (one server per 12-15 covers vs casual 25-30). Higher labor as a function of the service model.

**Steakhouse:** 32-36% target (median: 36-38%)

Slightly lower than fine dining because the protein focus simplifies the BOH operation (less garde manger, less pastry).

**Coffee shop / café:** 28-34% target (median: 34-36%)

Espresso operations need barista skill but have low average check. The math is sensitive to volume.

**Pizza (delivery + takeout heavy):** 25-30% target (median: 28-30%)

Low front-of-house labor (no service), lean BOH, high check velocity. Naturally low labor segment.

**Bakery:** 30-36% target (median: 35-37%)

Production labor is heavy. Most of the labor cost goes to prep before the doors open. Sales per labor hour matters more here than labor percentage.

**Sports bar / pub:** 28-34% target (median: 32-34%)

Higher beverage mix (lower labor allocation per dollar of revenue) helps. Heavy weekend skew puts pressure on scheduling discipline.

**Bar-only (no kitchen):** 18-24% target (median: 22-25%)

The lowest-labor segment in restaurant operations. One bartender can run $400/hour in sales during a peak rush.

## By state (2026 cost structure)

Geography moves labor cost more than concept does within a given segment.

**Highest labor cost states** (2-6 points above national segment median):
- California
- New York
- Washington
- Oregon
- Massachusetts
- Connecticut
- New Jersey
- Hawaii
- Alaska

**Lowest labor cost states** (2-4 points below national segment median):
- Texas
- Florida
- Georgia
- Tennessee
- Alabama
- South Carolina
- Mississippi
- Louisiana
- Oklahoma

The cost driver in high-states: higher base wages, mandatory paid sick, higher SUTA and workers comp rates, no tip credit allowed (in most). The cost driver in low-states: lower base wages, tip credit allowed at $2.13/hour for tipped workers, lower mandated benefits.

A casual full-service operation running 33% labor in California is on par with a similar operation running 28% in Texas. Same operational efficiency, different geography.

## Tipped vs non-tipped labor

The labor cost equation shifts based on whether you have tipped employees and whether you can take a tip credit.

**Tip credit states (federal $2.13/hr base for tipped workers):**
- Server hourly labor cost: $2.13/hr + payroll burden = ~$2.78/hr effective
- Tipped labor is 3-5x cheaper than non-tipped equivalent
- Total restaurant labor cost can run 4-8 points lower

**Non-tip-credit states (CA, NV, OR, WA, MN, AK, MT):**
- Server hourly labor cost: full state minimum wage + burden
- In California: $16-17/hr + 30% burden = ~$22/hr effective
- Total restaurant labor cost runs 4-8 points higher

This is why the same restaurant concept can be sustainable in Texas and unsustainable in California without different operational models. California operations typically need to run counter-service or limited table service to make the labor math work.

## Where you actually want to land

The discipline target: **NRA segment median minus 4-6 points**.

- Full-service in NRA-median geography: target 30-33%
- Limited-service: target 26-29%
- Adjust up 2-4 points for high-cost geography
- Adjust down 1-2 points for tip-credit-allowed geography with heavy tipped workforce

If you are 4+ points above the segment median, your operation is structurally over-labored. The fix is rarely "cut everyone an hour." It is usually:

1. Schedule against forecast, not against habit
2. Stagger shift starts to compress same-time arrivals
3. Cross-train so one person can cover multiple roles
4. Forecast labor weekly and cap before payroll closes

For each of these in detail, see [How to Reduce Labor Cost Without Cutting Hours](/blog/reduce-labor-cost-without-cutting-hours/).

## What to do today

Pull last month's labor cost. Apply the burden multiplier (25-35% depending on state). Find your segment above. Compare. If you are within 2 points of the operational target, you are running tight. If you are 4+ points above, the structural fix is the priority.

The fix is not a wage cut. It is a scheduling discipline. Most operations have 8-15% of labor hours that produce no revenue (over-staffed slow shifts, same-time arrivals, late cuts that miss the slowdown). That is the fixable portion.
