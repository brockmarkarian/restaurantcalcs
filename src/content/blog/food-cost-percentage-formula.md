Every operator knows the food cost formula. Most run it wrong. Not the math. The inputs. The formula is simple. The inputs are where the trap lives.

## The formula

> **Food Cost % = COGS ÷ Food Sales × 100**
>
> **COGS = Beginning Inventory + Purchases − Ending Inventory**

That is the standard. Toast, Lightspeed, Restaurant365 all teach it this way. The math is correct. The math is also incomplete.

The version that actually predicts your P&L:

> **True COGS = Beginning Inventory + Purchases + Transfers In − Transfers Out − Employee Meals − Comps − Ending Inventory**

The four extra adjustments are what separate "COGS as reported" from "COGS as actually consumed in service." Skip them and your food cost number is off by 1-4 points on a typical week (Restaurant365, MarginEdge).

## What each adjustment does

**Transfers in / out.** If your kitchen pulls $200 of produce from the bar's garnish station for a special, that is a transfer in to the kitchen. The receiving department got cost they didn't sell; the giving department lost cost without revenue. Both sides need adjustment or both sides report wrong food cost.

**Employee meals.** Staff meals cost real food but don't generate revenue. Pulling them out of COGS gives you the cost-of-sales-only number. Industry-average employee meal cost runs 1-3% of food sales (Toast).

**Comps and voids.** A comp is food you served and didn't get paid for. It belongs in marketing or guest-recovery cost, not food cost. Pulling comps out of food COGS makes the number a cleaner operational signal.

Skip these adjustments and your reported 32% food cost is really 29.5%. That sounds like a good thing, your real cost is lower than you thought. The problem is the *trend*. If employee meals jumped from 1.5% to 3% because the new sous chef is letting the line cooks eat whatever they want, your reported food cost stays flat at 32% but actual operational food cost is climbing. You will not see the trend unless you adjust.

## A worked example

Casual full-service restaurant, one-week period.

- Beginning food inventory: $12,400
- Food purchases (invoices booked this week): $24,800
- Ending food inventory: $11,900
- Transfers in (from bar to kitchen): $150
- Transfers out (kitchen to bar for syrup, garnish, citrus): $480
- Employee meals: $620
- Comps and voids: $310
- Food sales for the week: $86,400

**Reported COGS** (basic formula): $12,400 + $24,800 − $11,900 = **$25,300**

**Reported food cost**: $25,300 ÷ $86,400 = **29.3%**

**True COGS** (full formula): $25,300 + $150 − $480 − $620 − $310 = **$24,040**

**True food cost**: $24,040 ÷ $86,400 = **27.8%**

The 1.5-point difference is not noise. On annualized $4.5M revenue at 60% food sales share, that is **$40,500/year** of cost that lives in a category nobody is watching. Multiply by every operation that doesn't adjust and it explains why so much of the industry sits at the top end of the healthy food cost band, they are reading the wrong number.

## The 28-35% target and what changes it

Industry-standard food cost benchmark for casual full-service: **28-35%** (Toast, Lightspeed, NRA Industry Factbook).

Concept shifts the band:

- **Pizza:** 20-28% (cheese and dough run cheap; sauce is essentially free)
- **Burgers / QSR:** 25-32%
- **Casual full-service:** 28-35%
- **Steakhouse:** 32-40%
- **Sushi / seafood:** 30-38% (perishability + high-cost protein)
- **Italian (non-pizza):** 28-34%
- **Mexican:** 28-32%
- **Fine dining:** 32-40%
- **Bakery / coffee shop:** 28-35% (depends heavily on espresso vs pastry mix)

The right number for your concept is whatever leaves enough margin after labor, rent, and overhead to hit your net profit goal. For most independents that means landing 2-4 points below the segment median. If casual full-service runs 32% median, your operating target is 28-30%.

## Three reasons your number is wrong even when the math is right

**1. Bad inventory counting.** The biggest single error source. Counting frequency matters less than counting *consistently*. Weekly counts done on Sunday night at 9pm by the same person using the same form are reliable. Monthly counts done at random times by whoever is available are not.

The other counting failure: not counting open product correctly. A half-empty case of romaine is not zero. A quarter-bottle of olive oil is not zero. A bin of dry pasta with a guess at the level is a guess, not a count. Build the inventory sheet with weight scales for the items that matter, eyeball levels for the rest.

**2. Invoice timing.** If you book the $4,200 protein order on Thursday but the meat arrives Friday at midnight (already in next week's inventory), you have inflated this week's purchases by $4,200 and your COGS reads $4,200 high. Booking by receipt date, not invoice date, fixes this.

**3. Recipe non-compliance.** Your recipes might call for 4 oz of cheese on a burger. Your line might be pulling 5 oz. Your spec sheet is honest. Your operational reality is 25% over. Theoretical food cost stays at spec. Actual food cost runs hot. The gap shows up as variance, which is why every food cost discipline conversation eventually ends with "you need to run a variance check."

## What to do today

If you have never run the adjusted formula, pull last week's numbers and run it both ways. The gap between the simple formula and the adjusted formula is the cost category you have been ignoring. For most operations that gap is 1-3 points of food cost, which on a $1.5M food revenue base is $15-45K annually.

The fix is not complicated. It is just a checklist. Inventory consistently. Book purchases by receipt date. Track employee meals and comps separately. Run the full formula weekly. The discipline is harder than the math.
