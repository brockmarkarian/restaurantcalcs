The pour cost question every operator asks: what should it be? The pour cost answer most consultants give: 20%. That answer is wrong because beer, wine, and spirits run on very different cost structures and need separate targets. The blended number is useful for a back-of-envelope check, but the category-level numbers are what tell you where the leak lives.

## Why a single target is wrong

A bar that does 50% beer, 25% wine, 25% spirits on a 20% blended pour cost target might be running 26% on beer (bad), 24% on wine (slightly hot), and 18% on spirits (great). The blended number averages out to look healthy while two of three categories are leaking.

The right discipline is category-level targets with the blended number as a cross-check.

## Spirits / liquor

**Industry target:** 14-18% pour cost (Backbar, BinWise, Sculpture Hospitality)

Spirits are the highest-margin beverage category in your operation. A $24 bottle of well vodka yields about 15 servable 1.5 oz drinks after spillage. At $1.60 cost per drink and a $9 menu price, that is 17.8% pour cost. Even with 2-point variance from over-pour and comps, well drinks should land below 20%.

**Premium spirits run higher:** A $48 bottle of mid-shelf bourbon at $12 per drink price gives you $3.20 cost per drink (15 drinks per bottle), or 26.7% pour cost. Premium spirits naturally run higher because the markup multiple is lower at premium price points.

**Top-shelf is closer to wine economics:** $120 bottle of single-malt scotch at $22 per drink price = $8 cost per drink = 36% pour cost. The economics flip at premium price points because customers won't pay 7x markup on a $120 bottle.

**Where the pour cost leaks:** Over-pour on free-pour systems (1.75-2 oz actual vs 1.5 oz spec), comps and buy-backs, "shift drinks" at end of night, broken glass.

## Beer

**Bottle/can beer target:** 24-30% pour cost (Beverage Industry Magazine, Provi)

Bottle beer has a lower markup ratio than spirits because the unit cost is more visible to the customer. A $4.50 wholesale six-pack of premium domestic is $0.75 per bottle. Sold at $6 retail, that is 12.5% pour cost. Sold at $5 retail (lower-end pricing), 15%. Sold at $4 retail (volume bar), 18.75%.

Imports and craft beer in bottles run higher pour cost because the wholesale cost is higher and the customer is less price-tolerant. A $32 wholesale case of import six-packs is $1.33/bottle, sold at $8 = 16.6% pour cost. Sold at $6.50 (the typical neighborhood pub price) = 20.5%.

**Draft beer target:** 18-24% pour cost (Brewers Association, Toast)

Draft is more profitable than bottle on a per-ounce basis but adds operational complexity (lines, foam, cleaning, system maintenance). A typical half-barrel keg at $200 wholesale yields ~115 servable 16 oz pints after foam and waste. Cost per pint: $1.74. At $7 retail, pour cost is 24.9%. At $8 retail (premium positioning), 21.7%.

**Where the pour cost leaks:** Foam loss (regulator pressure issues), line cleaning waste, end-of-keg waste, free pours, wrong glassware.

For the full draft beer math, see [Half-Barrel Pints, Foam Loss, and What a Draft Beer Actually Costs](/blog/half-barrel-pints-foam-loss/).

## Wine

**Wine by the glass (BTG) target:** 22-30% pour cost (Provi, BinWise)

A 750mL bottle is 25.4 oz. At a 5 oz pour, that is 5 glasses per bottle. Spec on a 4 oz glass: 6 glasses. Most operations run 5 oz as the operational standard.

A $14 wholesale wine bottle yielding 5 glasses sold at $11 each: $2.80 cost per glass on $11 sale = 25.5% pour cost. Healthy.

The same bottle at a $14 menu price (lower-end pricing): 20% pour cost. Aggressive.

**Wine by the bottle target:** 28-35% pour cost (BinWise, Provi)

Bottle service has lower markup ratios than BTG. A $14 wholesale wine bottle sold at $42 is 33% pour cost. A $35 wholesale bottle sold at $95 is 36.8%. The customer expectation of "fair markup on a bottle" is roughly 2.5-3x wholesale, which sets the natural pour cost ceiling.

**Premium and reserve bottles:** Run higher pour cost (35-45%) because the price tolerance for markup is lower at premium tiers. A $180 reserve bottle marked up to $450 (2.5x) is 40% pour cost.

**Where the pour cost leaks:** Over-pour on BTG (5.5 oz vs 5 oz spec), oxidation waste on slow-moving bottles by the glass, broken cork charges absorbed by the house.

## Cocktails

**Cocktail target (blended):** 18-22% pour cost (Backbar, BevSpot)

Cocktails are spirits-driven with mixers, garnish, and ice as additional cost. A 1.5 oz pour of vodka ($1.60), 0.5 oz triple sec ($0.18), 0.75 oz lime juice ($0.10), and garnish ($0.05) = $1.93 total cost. Sold at $12 = 16% pour cost. Sold at $10 = 19.3%.

**Premium cocktails run higher:** A craft cocktail with 2 oz of bourbon ($3.50), housemade syrup ($0.20), garnish ($0.30) = $4 cost. Sold at $14 = 28.6%. Acceptable for premium positioning.

**Where the pour cost leaks:** Over-pour on the base spirit, garnish waste (the orange wedge that sat in the dish for three hours), juice waste (fresh-squeezed gone bad), recipe drift (bartender's "house style" using more spirit than spec).

## Total beverage program

**Blended target:** 18-24% pour cost

This is the aggregate across spirits, beer, wine, and cocktails. The healthy range depends on your menu mix.

**Heavy spirit / cocktail mix** (modern bar, craft cocktail bar): 16-20% blended
**Even split spirits / beer / wine:** 18-24% blended
**Heavy beer mix** (sports bar, craft beer focus): 22-28% blended
**Heavy wine mix** (wine bar, wine-program restaurant): 22-28% blended

If your blended number falls outside these bands for your mix, the category-level breakdown tells you which is off.

## How to read the blended number

A blended pour cost of 22% can hide very different stories.

**Story 1 (healthy):** 17% spirits, 23% beer, 26% wine, blended 22%. Each category at the top of its healthy range. No one leak; tighten across the board for marginal improvement.

**Story 2 (one category leaking):** 17% spirits, 31% beer, 24% wine, blended 22% (beer is over-weighted in this mix). The beer leak is doing the damage. Fix beer; spirits and wine are fine.

**Story 3 (cocktail problem):** 26% cocktails (heavy menu mix), 15% spirits (well drinks), 22% beer, 28% wine, blended 22%. The cocktail program is the issue, but the spirits-only number (well + premium) is healthy. The fix is cocktail-recipe-specific.

The blended number tells you whether you have a problem. The category-level numbers tell you where it lives. The variance against the category target tells you how much margin you are losing.

## What to do today

Pull last month's bar sales broken out by category (beer / wine / spirits / cocktails) if your POS supports it. Pull invoices by category. Calculate pour cost separately for each. Compare against the targets above. The category furthest above target is where the work is.

If your POS doesn't break out beverage sales by category, that is the first fix. Without category-level visibility, you are tracking the symptom (blended pour cost) instead of the diagnosis (which category is leaking).
